# Visual Style Guide

Codec_Tactics uses a single visual metaphor: a fiber lattice under live intrusion.

The game world is not a board with pieces on top. It is a maintained digital infrastructure layer. Nodes are service junctions, links are fiber routes, energy is available signal budget, ownership is routing authority, and corruption is hostile signal fracture. The network should be recognizable before the title is visible.

## Artistic Vision

The active style is fiber-optic infrastructure with tactical instrumentation.

Every screen should feel like the player is operating a living backbone:

- Connections are the hero because they carry signal, risk, and control.
- Nodes are physical junction hardware, not abstract tokens.
- The HUD is an attached diagnostics rail, not a separate software window.
- Motion communicates flow, pressure, instability, or confirmation.
- Brightness is reserved for actionable signal, objective state, corruption pressure, and mission results.

Avoid mixing in unrelated visual metaphors such as space holograms, generic neon dashboards, fantasy runes, neural tissue, or ornamental sci-fi panels.

## World Rules

- A node is a hardened junction in the lattice.
- A network connection is a bundled route with visible inner signal lanes.
- Energy is usable routing budget, shown as clean controlled signal.
- Corruption is hostile signal fracture that splinters across junction hardware.
- Ownership is routing authority: player control stabilizes links, corruption scars them, neutral nodes stay dim and uncommitted.
- A Relay is an amplifier tower that broadcasts in triangular directions.
- A Firewall is a shielded choke point with layered barriers.
- A Resource is a capacitor junction that stores and releases routing budget.
- The Objective is a keystone service that must remain controlled long enough to synchronize.

The physical rule is simple: signal moves through routed fiber bundles, junctions shape or resist that signal, and corruption damages routes by forcing noisy fractures through them.

## Color Language

- Player control: green-teal, stable and grounded.
- Active signal: pale mint, used sparingly for packets and core highlights.
- Relay amplification: aqua-teal, brighter than normal control but cooler than objective signal.
- Resource energy: warm amber, used for stored budget and resource identity.
- Objective: yellow gold, the strongest positive attention color.
- Firewall: muted violet, defensive and structured.
- Corruption: red-magenta, reserved for enemy control, fracture marks, loss, and hostile intent.
- Warning: orange, used for instability and pressure before full corruption.
- Neutral: gray-green, subdued and low contrast.
- Background: near-black green-blue, quiet enough to keep the network readable.

Do not let the whole screen become one hue family. The dominant read should be dark infrastructure, mint signal, amber objectives/resources, and red corruption.

## Shape Language

Silhouette is the first identifier. Color and icons support the silhouette.

- Player core: octagonal socket with concentric center and four route ports.
- Standard node: hexagonal junction.
- Resource: diamond capacitor with internal charge bars.
- Relay: triangular amplifier with three broadcast arms.
- Firewall: shield plate with stacked barrier lines.
- Objective: hexagonal keystone with an internal shield/cross structure.
- Corruption: hostile fracture slashes over the base silhouette.
- Selection and hover: octagonal inspection brackets.
- Valid action: diamond routing bracket.

Text labels should explain details on hover or in the diagnostics rail, but the board must remain readable without text inside every node.

## Network Rendering

Links should look like infrastructure routes, not generic glowing lines.

- Active connections use bundled lanes with a dark outer casing, glassy fiber body, and a pale signal core.
- Packets move directionally as small diamond signal carriers with short trails.
- Relay links get additional moving amplifier cells.
- Inactive connections stay visible but low-energy, with weak side fibers and mild static.
- Enemy influence turns routes red or orange only where corruption participates.

The connection layer should occupy the player's attention before individual nodes do.

## Background

The background is distant infrastructure behind the playable lattice.

Use subtle scanlines, diagonal buried fiber paths, distant small junctions, and low-opacity atmosphere. It should imply depth without competing with gameplay state. Avoid decorative noise that makes valid targets, corruption pressure, or objective state harder to read.

## User Interface

The HUD is a diagnostics rail connected to the lattice.

- Keep it compact and attached to the edge.
- Prefer rails, signal bands, and small state markers over heavy boxes.
- Use short labels and readable hierarchy.
- Keep action controls visible and direct.
- Keep the board dominant.

The HUD may use text because it reports precise state, but it should not explain the visual language in-game.

## Animation Principles

Animation must communicate intent.

- Packet motion means live traffic.
- Relay pulses mean amplification.
- Objective pulses mean synchronization progress.
- Orange pulse means instability or pressure.
- Red fracture motion means corruption activity.
- Selection and hover easing mean inspection focus.
- Shockwaves mean state changes.

Remove or reduce animation that does not describe flow, ownership, danger, action feedback, or mission state.

## Lighting Principles

Lighting should be functional.

- Outer glows identify ownership and pressure.
- Inner highlights identify signal-bearing elements.
- Background lighting remains low contrast.
- The strongest light belongs to objective state, selected state, valid actions, or mission result.

## Effects Philosophy

Effects are tactical language, not decoration. A new effect should answer one of these questions:

- Where is signal moving?
- What can I act on?
- What changed?
- What is threatened?
- What is synchronized?
- What belongs to me?
- What has been corrupted?

If an effect cannot answer one of those questions, it does not belong in the base presentation.

## Audio Direction

Audio should sound like routed signal and physical network hardware.

- Hover/select: small optical switch ticks.
- Confirm/capture: clean route lock or junction handshake.
- Reinforce: stable low-frequency bracing tone.
- Weaken: filtered scrape or desync cut.
- Corruption: clipped fracture noise, not generic monster or alarm sound.
- Objective: warm synchronization pulse.
- Ambient: layered low hum that rises with pressure but stays under the board.

Synthesized audio is acceptable as long as it supports the fiber-lattice identity.
